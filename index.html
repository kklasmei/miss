Tips



TRANSPARENCY: There’s a strong argument to be made for transparency not just for women, but for minorities and other
disadvantaged groups. African-American men earn less than white men, for example, though a Harvard Business Review
analysis found that controlling for education, black men out-earn both white and black women. Evidence also suggests
that less secrecy about pay results in greater employee loyalty and lower turnover.

SALARY MARKET: For yourself and for people on your team (both women and men), you want to make sure your salaries are
marked to market. If there are people who have been in the company for years, their compensation may have fallen behind.
Many people don’t even think to negotiate for more in the jobs they already have. Be the proactive one, get current salary data,
and if it supports higher compensation, share it with management. You may just help, not only yourself, but your colleagues get a raise.

NEGOTIATION: If you are comfortable with negotiating, help others do the same. Let your colleagues know how you find salary
information. Encourage your colleagues to prepare for their performance reviews and to ask for more. Share articles on negotiating.
If you know of a colleague preparing for a raise request, role play with them and give them some live practice with the negotiation.

Act



Visit the website of the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC). The EEOC enforces the Equal Pay Act and
Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. You can learn about your rights and find out how and when to file a charge of discrimination
with the EEOC. For federal sector employment, you also can refer to your agency’s federal sector complaint procedures.
